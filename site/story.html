The Story

Every era of the web redefined identity. The agentic web is next.

The web has reinvented itself roughly once a decade — and each time, the hardest question was the same: who is on the other end, and what are they allowed to do? Autonomous AI agents are forcing that question again, at machine speed and across every boundary at once. This is the inflection point.

The moment we're in Read the model
Web 1.0 — Read

The static web

Pages you could read, not change. Visitors were anonymous; there was no identity layer at all. Trust meant "the document loaded."

Web 2.0 — Read / Write

The platform web

Accounts, logins, and the social graph. Identity became the human login — secured by OAuth 2.0, OpenID Connect, and enterprise SSO. Powerful, but built entirely around a person clicking a button.

Web 3.0 — Read / Write / Own

The decentralized web

Self-sovereign identity: W3C Decentralized Identifiers (DIDs) and Verifiable Credentials let a subject prove who they are and what they hold — portably, without a single central authority.

Now — Read / Write / Act

The agentic web

Software that decides and acts on its own, calls tools, spawns sub-agents, and crosses organizations — faster than any human can oversee. The actor is no longer a person at all.

What Web3 unlocks

The building blocks the agentic web inherits

Web3's real contribution isn't tokens or chains — it's a way to carry identity and authority that no longer depends on a single provider. Those are exactly the primitives an agent needs to operate across organizations it has never met.

Self-owned identifiers

W3C DIDs give an agent a globally unique identifier it controls with its own keys — verifiable by a third party that has no visibility into its host (§3.1, "Sovereign and Portable Agent Identity").

Portable proof

Verifiable Credentials let an agent prove a claim — "authorized to access this" — without a central lookup, with selective disclosure so it reveals only what's needed.

Decentralized trust

Federation and cryptographic verification let agents establish trust on first contact across domains where no shared identity provider exists.

Attenuable capabilities

Object-capability tokens (Biscuits, Macaroons) embed authority in the credential itself and can be narrowed offline — the basis for safe recursive delegation (§3.2).

The significance of this moment

For the first time, the actor isn't human

Every prior identity system — OAuth, OIDC, SSO, even DIDs — was designed for a human subject. Agents break that assumption. They act on behalf of people but are distinct from them; they delegate to other agents; they operate across trust domains where no single identity provider has authority. The OpenID Foundation's Identity Management for Agentic AI (2025) names the stakes: avoid agent identity fragmentation, replace impersonation with true delegation, and give every agent a governable, auditable lifecycle. Get this right and the Internet of Agents is trustworthy by construction; get it wrong and we inherit an ecosystem of unaccountable, high-velocity actors.

Impersonation → delegation

An agent should prove it acts on behalf of a principal while staying distinct from them — a clear, auditable chain.

Permissions that narrow

Authority must attenuate down a delegation chain, never expand — least privilege enforced, not hoped for.

A verifiable lifecycle

Provision, verify, govern, and — critically — verifiably de-provision. A half-revoked agent is a standing threat.

Accountability by default

Every action ties back to both the human principal and the agent actor, closing the audit gap.

What it means for people

Your digital twin — and a web where anyone can earn

Give a person a verifiable agent and you give them a digital twin: a tireless representative that acts on their behalf, yet stays cryptographically distinct and accountable to them — never an impersonation. That twin can do more than fetch information; with a real identity and bounded, delegated authority it can transact. Signed payment mandates and "Know Your Agent" flows (whitepaper §3.6) let an agent buy, sell, and settle on your behalf within limits you set. The result is a web where anyone — not just platforms — can put an agent to work and earn, because the agent can be trusted, scoped, and audited.

A twin, not a mask

The agent represents you but is identifiably distinct — every action traces back to both you and it.

Authority with limits

Delegated scope that only narrows: your twin acts within an envelope you define, enforced, not assumed.

An economy it can join

Verifiable identity + signed mandates let agents pay and get paid — opening earning to everyone, not just incumbents.

Our answer

Treat agents as first-class identities

It composes the eras rather than discarding them — OIDC from Web 2.0, DIDs and Verifiable Credentials from Web 3.0 — into a single lifecycle for the agentic web.

The pattern: Linux → Kubernetes → AI

Every compute era grows an open control plane — and an identity layer

The same shape keeps repeating. An open foundation standardizes the substrate; an open control plane orchestrates what runs on it; and each new class of workload needs a way to prove what it is and what it may do.

Linux

The open substrate

An open kernel standardized compute itself — the neutral foundation everything else was built on.

Kubernetes

The open control plane

It orchestrated containerized workloads and, with SPIFFE/SPIRE, gave each workload a verifiable, short-lived identity — no shared secrets.

AI agents

The new workload

Autonomous, non-deterministic, cross-domain. They are the next class of workload — and they need the same neutral identity layer, raised to handle delegation and behavior.

Agent Identity is that layer for the agentic era: a vendor-neutral, standards-grounded identity control plane that extends workload identity (SPIFFE) with delegation, verifiable credentials, lifecycle, and audit — so agents are as governable as the containers Kubernetes orchestrates.

And like Linux, this is ultimately about freedom. An open, neutral identity layer is what keeps the Internet of Agents open — interoperable by default, owned by no single vendor, and free of the lock-in that fragmentation creates. The whitepaper is blunt about the stakes: for this ecosystem to thrive, it must not become a walled garden.

Composed, not walled

Open innovation
